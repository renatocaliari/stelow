# Stage 7: Evolve to a Viable Business

**Objective**: transform accumulated learning into a sustainable business. After confirming the problem, the solution, and delivery capability, the focus is on **business risk**.

The search is not for a final state of "success", but for a **dynamic equilibrium** between four key areas:

1. **Market-Product Fit**: ensure that the product solves a real problem and there is demand in the market.
2. **Model-Market Fit**: ensure that the business model is viable and sustainable in the market.
3. **Channel-Model Fit**: ensure that sales, marketing, and delivery channels are optimized for the business model.
4. **Product-Channel Fit**: ensure that channels are suitable for selling and delivering the product efficiently.

---

## Synthesizing Learnings with an Evolutionary Lens

When transforming the "valuable manual process" into a first version of the product, the path is rarely a straight line. It looks more like **crossing a river by stepping on stones** — each step reveals the best position for the next one.

Instead of fixing on a final destination, be inspired by evolution: it doesn't follow a predefined objective, but explores what is interesting from the current point. The intention is to find the most promising **"stepping stone"**, which opens the largest number of new paths possible.

### An Evolutionary Lens for the First Version

Reflection questions (inspired by evolutionary product principles):

- **Flexibility and exaptation**: does this first version allow uses we didn't predict? Can it be "exapted" to solve other problems in the future?
- **Connection potential (solution space)**: does this solution serve as a foundation and facilitate the development of other features in the future?
- **Ecosystem (gene flow)**: how easy would it be for this solution to connect to other tools the audience already uses? Are we building something that can live well in a larger ecosystem?
- **Competitive advantage (local competition)**: does this approach position us in a specific niche that is difficult to imitate, instead of direct and broad competition?

## Translating the Process into Product: Your First "Stepping Stone"

Based on the "valuable manual process" and with value and commitment risks already mitigated, the challenge is to decide **what to systematize or automate first**. Each choice is a "stepping stone" that optimizes a different aspect of the future business.

### Possible Approaches:

**Systematize the Core Value (The "Minimum Version")**
Automate only the most critical and unique part of the process, the one that generates the greatest perceived value for the customer. Release a system with a single essential feature to a small group. Tests execution risk at scale with minimum engineering effort.

**Build the Acquisition Engine**
Instead of focusing on the product itself, build a feature or tool whose main purpose is to attract new customers. It can be a referral system, shareable content generated by the product, or a free tool that serves as a lure. Tests business model and channel risk.

**Create an Internal Support Tool**
Instead of building the solution for the customer, develop a small product that automates a painful part of your own manual process. Increases efficiency and margin, and can eventually become a product for customers.

**Develop in Partnership (The "Pilot Solution")**
Find a strategic client or partner and co-develop the first version focused on their needs. It's the essence of "creating a pilot solution" — a focused implementation that serves as an in-depth test case. Validates viability in a real scenario, can guarantee initial revenue, and mitigates the risk of building something that doesn't fit into a real workflow.

## When Building the Version

Objective: **decrease your own effort to deliver value**:

- **Systematization and automation**: evaluate the gain of using AI assistance, code or low-code to automate the most critical parts of the process discovered in the previous stage.
- **Simplification and removal**: eliminate from the process (service or product) everything that didn't demonstrate clear value for the first users.
- **Strategic partnerships**: evaluate if forming partnerships can optimize or outsource parts of the process that are not your main focus.

Revisit the "nine components for a desirable solution" to ensure that this first version is valuable and easy to use.

**Create one or more case studies** with your first clients — serves as social proof and helps refine communication.

## Find and Focus on Acquisition Channels

1. Experiment with some promotion channels simultaneously and evaluate performance.
2. Select one or two channels with the best result (not necessarily the largest volume, but the **most aligned customers**) and double efforts on them.
3. After stabilizing one channel, start experimenting with others.

## What Risk Are We Mitigating?

- **Execution/realization risk** (feasibility — whether it's possible to build what's needed in the available time, skills, and technologies)
- **Usability risk** (whether people will know how to use the new solution)
- **Business risk** — basic viability: knowing if you're capable of making enough sales and generating enough revenue to cover operational expenses and generate profit.

---

## What to Observe (Signals)

These are not goals, but lenses to understand what's happening:

**Proxy metric**: what is the main action that indicates users are extracting value? E.g.: percentage of new customers who create their first project in the first week. Answers: *are people using the solution for its main purpose?*

**Retention rate**: how many customers continue using the solution over time? Answers: *do people keep coming back?*

**Customer Lifetime Value (CLTV) to Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) ratio**: in the long run, is the value generated by a customer greater than the cost to acquire them? Points to financial sustainability.

**Viral coefficient**: are your current customers bringing new customers? Answers: *does the solution spread organically?*

## It's Not the End — Evolutionary Product

This stage is not the end, but the beginning of a new learning cycle, now with a more complete system in operation. Experimentation continues, focused on optimizing and adapting the business to the constantly changing environment.

The **evolutionary product criteria** continue to be a valuable compass: they help evaluate each new feature or adjustment not just by its immediate merit, but by the potential to open new paths and strengthen the product's resilience in the long term.
