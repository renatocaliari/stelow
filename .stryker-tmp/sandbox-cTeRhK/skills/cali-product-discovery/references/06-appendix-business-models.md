## ⚙️ Business Models

Creativity triggers from Strategyn (Outcome-Driven Innovation / Jobs to Be Done) to adapt and experiment with new business models.

### To Reduce Variable Costs

1. Pass a cost entirely to the customer (self-serve, assemble, install, maintain the product, etc.)
2. Pass an expense to suppliers (inventory tracking sensors, customization of supplies/packaging, etc.)
3. Pass an expense to distributors (acquire and install optional features, maintain the product, store inventory, etc.)
4. Turn a cost into revenue (charge for a sales call, etc.)
5. Turn waste into revenue (sell as byproduct, repurpose, reuse, etc.)
6. Get free supplies and materials (use someone else's waste as raw material, etc.)
7. Use free labor (volunteers, interns, non-profit organizations, academics, etc.)
8. Eliminate an essential business activity or process and reinvest the savings to gain competitive advantage.
9. Incorporate costs when they are at their lowest level (specific period of the year, surplus or liquidation sale, etc.)
10. Decrease a variable cost that forces a reduction in other variable costs.
11. Make costs more variable (hiring methods to suit costs to market conditions, etc.)
12. Reduce costs using existing infrastructure for multiple purposes.
13. Outsource non-essential activities performed inefficiently.
14. Acquire essential activities performed efficiently.
15. Own the infrastructure, not the content.
16. Incorporate more expenses in one area to cut even more expenses in another (bundles instead of tracking multiple packages, etc.)
17. Eliminate a cost through barter.
18. Make a charity contribution in exchange for necessary services.
19. Make a variable cost fixed.

### To Reduce Fixed Costs

1. Use existing resources wisely to eliminate capital investments (facilities, equipment, tools, etc.)
2. Reduce fixed costs by incurring them when they are lowest (when subsidized, when financial incentives are in place, etc.)
3. Use other people's money to pay for capital investments.
4. Own the content, not the infrastructure.
5. Leverage a capital investment made by someone else (intermediary, third party, etc.)
6. Pass a fixed cost to the community (regulatory fees, taxes, etc.)
7. Make a fixed cost variable.

### For Revenue Generation

1. Divert a revenue source from someone elsewhere in the value chain (supplier, distributor, installer, maintainer, etc.)
2. Identify resources inherent to the value delivery platform and sell/use them (information, energy, etc.)
3. Collect revenue that will coerce future revenue generation.
4. Accept a non-cash form of payment (credit card, subscription, royalties, etc.)
5. Accept a non-traditional form of ownership (share, earn, repeat, etc.)
6. Add a revenue source related to the product (optional features, different product line, replacement parts, etc.)
7. Add a revenue source related to the time of purchase (help the customer make the purchase, finance the product, etc.)
8. Add a revenue source related to support (maintenance, installation, training, storage, after-sales service, disposal, etc.)
9. Collect revenue from a different payer (insurer, distributor, advertiser, government, etc.)
10. Generate revenue from operational operations (charge admission for facilities/tours, sell training content to third parties, etc.)
11. Sell a consumable to collect revenue.
12. Increase revenue from charity sources (fundraising organization, non-profit organization.)
13. Collect revenue at a different point in time or at a different frequency.
14. Collect revenue during downtime.
15. Own the content and the infrastructure.

## 🔓 The Open Source Paradox: Delivering Value by Giving Up Control

### The Problem of the Proprietary Code Approach

The belief that value resides in the secrecy of code creates constant friction:
- The customer needs to trust a "black box"
- High cost to acquire customers (convincing that the secret solution is better)
- Slow feedback cycle and isolated development
- Software creation itself (especially low complexity) is becoming a commodity — with good developers and advanced AI, replicating features is faster than ever

### The Alternative Perspective

**The product is not the code — it's the living system.** The strategy shifts from protection to **curation**. Open source becomes the ideal vehicle for this, accelerating distribution and building trust — which become the true differentials, harder to replicate.

*Analogy*: publishing the recipe for a complex dish. The recipe (the code) can be copied. Other restaurants can even use the recipe. But the value of the original restaurant lies in the consistency of execution, in the atmosphere, in the trust that customers have, and — perhaps most importantly — in the **evolution of the menu**. The hardest work may not be adding new dishes, but knowing which ones to remove to maintain essence and quality. The free recipe didn't cannibalize the business; it **created the market**.

### Business Models with Open Source

**Open-core**: the core software (which solves the problem for most users) is open and free. Monetization comes from "premium" closed-source features that solve corporate niche problems (advanced security, integrations, administration tools).

**Managed Hosting (Software as a Service)**: sell convenience. "You can host this yourself, configure, update, worry about security. Or you can pay us a monthly fee to do it all for you." Examples: WordPress.com (vs. WordPress.org), GitLab.

**Consulting, Support, and Training**: the software is 100% free, but using it at scale requires deep knowledge. The company that created it sells that knowledge in the form of technical support, consulting, and training. Example: Red Hat on top of Linux.

**Alternative Licenses (Fair Code, Source Available)**: hybrid models. Example: n8n — open source but with a license that restricts commercial use by third parties who want to offer n8n as a competing direct service. Attempt to have open source transparency without the risk of predatory competition. Elastic and MongoDB also followed similar paths.

**Paid Access to Repositories or Builds**: works like a "sponsorship" model, where companies pay to have access to more stable builds or private repository with priority fixes.

### Organizational Structures for Open Source Projects

**For-Profit Organizations and Venture Capital (VC)**: massive distribution and trust generated by open source can be the basis for accelerated growth. ⚠️ Pressure for quick financial return can conflict with building a long-term business and community interests. Pressure for "exit" can lead to decisions that prioritize short-term profit.

**Foundations or Non-Profit Associations**: in Brazil, projects can be maintained by association or foundation, supported by donations from companies and the community. Reinforces trust that the project serves the public interest. The Public Ministry oversees foundations to ensure they follow their statutory objectives.

**Other Structures**:
- *Platform cooperativism*: the users and developers themselves own the platform, aligning incentives.
- *Steward-ownership*: the company cannot be sold as an asset. Profits are reinvested in the purpose, and voting control remains in the hands of "guardians" committed to the mission, not to financial value extraction.

### How to Protect Yourself from Competition with Open Source

1. **The Brand and Trust**: people tend to trust the original creators. The brand becomes synonymous with authenticity and quality.
2. **Distribution and the Ecosystem**: a competitor can copy the code, but not the community — network of users, contributors, plugins, and integrations. It's the most effective protective moat.
3. **The Wisdom of System Evolution**: ability to evolve the system with **discipline to keep it simple**. A competitor can copy today's features, but not the criteria that will define tomorrow's product.
4. **Defining the Market Standard**: a successful project can become the practical standard in its category. The switching cost to a competing tool (even if technically similar) becomes very high due to the established ecosystem.

### Experiments for Those Who Want to Explore This Path

- **Internal Library Experiment**: transform a useful (but not-critical) internal component into an open source project to learn the dynamics.
- **"Paid Convenience" Experiment**: offer a small layer of paid convenience on an existing project (installer or premium documentation).
- **Minimum Open-Core Experiment**: release a "core" version of a product and see if this drives interest in the paid version with advanced features.

## 🏷 Pricing

Thinking about pricing is thinking about the nature of the relationship we want to build. Price is not just a number; it's the **formalization of a value exchange** — an agreement on how risk and benefit are shared.

### Models — Theme: Exchange Base

**One-Time Payment for Ownership — "Buy It, It's Yours"**
Definitive transaction. The customer pays once and assumes total ownership and responsibility. The relationship between creator and buyer is intense at the moment of sale and then dissolves (except for warranties/support). Works well for durable goods or digital products where the maintenance cost for the creator is zero after sale.

**Payment for Access (Instead of Ownership)**
The logic is the exchange of permanent ownership for temporary access. Manifests in main forms:

1. **Access by Time Period**:
   - *Recurring subscription*: periodic payment (usually monthly) to maintain continuous access. Standard model for Netflix, Spotify, most SaaS.
   - *Finite term license*: one-time payment for access for a long and predetermined period (one or five years). At the end of the term, access ends without automatic renewal. Offers predictability for both sides.

2. **Access per Unit of Outcome**: payment is tied to a specific and transactional delivery. Example: AI service that transcribes audios — the customer pays R$ 0.10 per minute of transcribed audio. Pays for output, not for time of use.

⚠️ Don't confuse with "value-generated pricing": in access per unit of outcome, the price is fixed per transaction (X per minute, Y per image). In value-generated pricing, it's a variable percentage of a business impact (Z% of revenue increase).

### Models — Theme: Consumption Control

**Pre-Paid Credits Payment**
The customer buys a package of "credits" in advance, consumed as they use the service. Gives clear control over spending and guarantees upfront revenue for the company. Forces awareness about use — each action has a visible cost that deducts the balance, making consumption more intentional.

**Computational Granularity Pricing**
Driven by variable cost technologies like AI. Price is tied directly to the resource consumed in a very specific way: per seconds/minutes of time that an AI model uses a specialized processor (GPU/TPU), per token of text processed, or per image generated. Reflects the real economy behind the service. Transparent, but transfers cost variability to the customer.

### Models — Theme: Interest Alignment

**Performance Payment**
The provider assumes part of the client's risk. Payment is not tied to the product or time of use, but to the concrete result it generates. If the expected result is not achieved, payment is reduced or non-existent. Requires high trust and ability to measure "performance", but creates powerful alignment of interests: one's success is the other's success.

**Value-Generated Pricing**
Evolution of performance payment. Price is tied to a high-level business indicator — percentage of additional revenue or cost savings that the service provided. It's the deepest form of partnership, turning the provider into a partner in the result. Its measurement and negotiation complexity make it a path for few.

**Payment with Data**
The customer receives a discount or free access in exchange for permission for their data (ideally in an anonymous and secure way) to be used to train and improve the system, especially AI models. Formalizes a value exchange that often happens in a hidden way.

### Models — Theme: Combinations and New Frontiers

**Bundling**
Instead of selling items separately, group them in a "bundle" with a single price. Increases perceived value (the customer feels they get more for less) and simplifies the pain of purchase decision (a single transaction instead of several micro-transactions).

**Hybrid Pricing**
Combines stability with flexibility. Usually: a fixed subscription fee to guarantee access to the platform and basic set of features, plus a variable component for consumption of more expensive resources (AI credits, advanced features). Seeks balance: revenue predictability for the company and feeling of fairness for the customer.

### Application Strategies

**Stepped Pricing / Tiered Pricing — Pre-Sale/Launch Strategy**
Turns pricing into a participatory event, directly rewarding the trust and agility of the first adopters. The risk for the buyer is higher at the beginning (little social proof), so the price should be as low as possible at this moment. As more people join, risk decreases, social proof increases, and the price goes up.

*Example*:
- Initial batch: symbolic value (e.g., R$ 10) for the first buyers
- Following batches: with each new group of 50 people, the price increases by fixed amount (e.g., R$ 50) or percentage (e.g., 10%)

Benefits: creates strong sense of urgency; clear incentive not to "leave for later"; generates quick feedback cycle on demand; the speed with which batches sell out can indicate how correct the offer is and probe the price ceiling the audience is willing to pay in that context.

⚠️ Requires transparent communication — the increase rules must be clear to everyone from the beginning.

**Subscription Fatigue — Market Response Strategy**
We are in a phase of "subscription fatigue" — the accumulation of small monthly charges generates constant cognitive and financial burden for consumers. Alternatives:

- *Alternative billing cycles*: offer quarterly or annual — longer cycle usually comes with discount, rewards the customer for commitment, and improves company cash flow.
- *One-Time Payment (Lifetime Deal)*: return to one-time payment. The "buy once, use forever" proposal has powerful psychological appeal — eliminates recurrence anxiety and creates a sense of ownership. For the business, it can generate very strong initial cash flow, although long-term earning potential may be lower.
- *Long-Term License*: usage license for a determined time (one, two, or three years) with expressive discount for single upfront payment. It's the ideal field to apply the principles of the trust pact.

### Perception and Trust

The way price is presented has a profound impact on how it is perceived.

**Monetary Abstraction**: when presenting a price as "20" instead of "R$ 20.00", two sources of noise are removed: the dollar sign (explicit reminder of the act of spending) and cents (increase cognitive load without adding useful information). A clean and direct number tends to be processed more fluently, seeming psychologically "smaller".

**Magnitude Perception (Endings 5, 7, and 9)**: left-digit effect — the brain processes the first digit from the left as an anchor for magnitude perception. That's why 9.99 seems significantly cheaper than 10.00. The use of endings 5, 7, or 9 can also carry cultural connotation of "opportunity price" or "discount".

**Fluency (Abbreviations)**: for larger numbers, presenting "1.2k" instead of "1,200" shortens the number, making it visually lighter and faster to read, inserting it into a more digital and modern language.

**Time (Temporal Reframing)**: fractioning into smaller and more palatable time unit. Software that costs R$ 365 per year can be presented as "just R$ 1 per day". Anchors the cost to a trivial daily expense, making the total value less intimidating and easier to justify.

**Decoy (The Decoy Effect)**: introduce a third price option intentionally less attractive, to make one of the others look better in comparison.
*Example*:
- Plan A: R$ 50
- Plan B (decoy): R$ 90 (with almost the same benefits as Plan C)
- Plan C: R$ 100 (with much more benefits than Plan B) → Plan C, which previously seemed expensive, now looks like a bargain compared to Plan B.

**Sum of Parts (Partitioned Pricing)**: instead of a single price, the total cost is broken into smaller parts (e.g., R$ 80 for the product + R$ 20 for shipping instead of R$ 100 with free shipping). The bet is that the customer anchors on the lower base price. ⚠️ Requires care — if partitioning is not transparent, it can be perceived as a "hidden fee", generating distrust.

**Discount Frame**: the general rule is the **"Rule of 100"**:
- For products that cost less than 100: a percentage discount seems larger (e.g., 25% off an R$ 80 item sounds better than R$ 20 off)
- For products over 100: the absolute value seems more significant (e.g., R$ 250 off a R$ 1,000 item sounds better than 25% off)
The idea: use the frame that presents the largest nominal number.

**Recontextualization (Value Anchoring)**: instead of anchoring the price on direct competitors, anchor on the **value of the solution you replace**. Example: an AI agent that performs sales should not be compared to other software, but rather to the cost of a salesperson's salary or the revenue increase generated. The customer's question changes from "why does this software cost X and the other Y?" to "how much do I save (or earn) by using this solution instead of the traditional alternative?".

### The Trust Pact: An Anti-Lock-In Pricing Approach

In a saturated market, where customers feel increasingly trapped in services by contracts, inertia, or difficulty migrating data, **trust can be the biggest competitive differential**.

Building a "trust pact" means designing the commercial relationship in a way that gives power and autonomy to the customer. It rests on three principles:

**1. Contractual Freedom (The Right to Exit)**
The customer can end the relationship whenever they want, without bureaucratic barriers or punitive fines. In finite term license plans, the relationship ends by default, without automatic renewals. In recurring plans, cancellation is simple and immediate. *The message: the customer stays for the quality of the service, not for a contractual obligation.*

**2. Financial Freedom (Insurance Against Initial Risk)**
Ensures that if a customer changes their mind — especially in the first few months — they don't lose their entire investment. Mechanics: fair refund with the returned value deducting only the usage period calculated based on the standard price, without discount. The purpose: give the customer the peace of mind that a high-value purchase decision is **reversible** if it proves mistaken at the beginning.

**3. Informational Freedom (Data Belongs to the Customer)**
The exit barrier of a service cannot be the difficulty of taking their data away. An explicit commitment to **data portability** — exporting information easily, freely, and in an open format — is the recognition that the company offers a tool, but the work and creation belong to the user.

**Why Adopting These Principles is Also a Business Strategy**: a company that facilitates customer entry and exit is making a bold bet on the quality of its own product, signaling that it intends to retain customers **by merit, and not by lock-in**.
