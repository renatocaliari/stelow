## 🧲 Stimulating Marketplace: Supply and Demand

### 19 Tactics to Stimulate Marketplaces:

1. **Get the harder side first.** When the harder side (supply or demand) reaches its critical activity point, network effects kick in and value is created organically for the easier side. In most markets, either supply is harder to attract, or demand. Discovering this through the sales and onboarding process, typically the harder side is the most valuable — once you have enough of them, the other side is 2-3 times easier to bring aboard.

2. **Strongly attract to a niche and repeat.** Find the small groups in your community that care most about your market ("white-hot center") and go after them. Usually, this is discovered by going broad enough to collect data that shows the most activity.

3. **Subsidize the most valuable side of the market.** Pay in cash to the most valuable side of the market — or to the most valuable niche within the most valuable side — to join the marketplace.

4. **Make supply look bigger with automation.** Start the supply side by aggregating as much web data as possible to create a "perceived aura of activity".

5. **Build one side as an email list.** An easy and cheap way to start a market, especially if many buyers are also sellers and vice versa.

6. **Hold meetups and gatherings.** Hard to scale. In the beginning, however, they can be effective for generating community, demonstrating activity, and getting direct customer feedback — especially when you're not active on social media.

7. **Create a SaaS tool for one side of the market.** When you give or sell a SaaS tool to one side of the market, it helps keep them in the market, giving time to attract the other side.

8. **Provide software to third parties who can bring one side of the market.**

9. **Find a giant user for initial supply or demand.**

10. **Make only one side change their behavior.**

11. **Make something free suddenly.** Transforming something that used to cost money into something free will attract users.

12. **Create a product first and then open a market.**

13. **Connect the two sides manually.**

14. **Prefer markets where buyers are also sellers.** Avoid building a single-sided market altogether.

15. **Create exclusive access.** Restrict access and create fear of missing supply or demand can generate strong word of mouth and rush of participation when the idea goes viral. However, this almost never works. Examples: Gilt, Gmail, and Mailbox.

16. **Define a geographic constraint.** It's easier to generate a lot of activity when limiting the operating area at launch.

17. **Define a time constraint.** One can program enthusiasm into the market with time constraints.

18. **Define a demand constraint.**

19. **Pay users with tokens.** If you create a token for the market, you can pay people to join, even when there's no activity.

## 🧩 Developing Solutions and Strategies

### Micro Products

Types of micro products to explore:
- Directory list / directory marketplace
- Job board
- Small e-commerce boutique or antique shop
- Platform to connect local producers (physical product or service) with local or external buyers
- Forum or community
- Internet browser extension (e.g., Chrome Store, Firefox)
- Tools (embed app, plugins, or bots) for existing platforms (Telegram, WhatsApp, WordPress, Shopify, Slack, Discord, Salesforce, or others)
- API mashup: ideas that combine already available APIs
- Micro-SaaS with a specific feature
- Micro-app for mobile with a specific feature
- Two-sided marketplace
- Live event or workshop
- Consulting or mentoring
- Niche authority blog
- Affiliate marketing
- Service provision
- Dropshipping

### Product Strategies (Jobs to Be Done — 5 Types)

**Differentiated Strategy**: serve underserved consumers. Offers better performance in accomplishing the job, charging more for it. Offers unique benefits or features not found in other products, meeting specific needs in a differentiated way.
*Examples*: Uber Black, iPhone.

**Dominant Strategy**: serve all types of consumers (underserved and well-served). Offer better performance in accomplishing the job and charge less. Become the best solution in the category for a specific job, outperforming the competition in performance or value.
*Examples*: UberX, Netflix — more convenient and cheaper.

**Sustaining Strategy**: continue serving current customers with incremental improvements in existing products or services to continuously meet the constantly evolving needs of customers. No major differentiations in performance or price.

**Discreet Strategy**: serve consumers with restricted options. Charge more for limited or worse performance. Focus on niches or success criteria of ignored customers, attending to jobs that are not the main focus of large competitors.
*Examples*: food sales and ATM at airports; lending to indebted people.

**Disruptive Strategy**: serve over-served consumers and non-consumers. Charge less and with limited or worse performance. Introduce changes that redefine how a job is accomplished, usually making it cheaper or more accessible.
*Example*: Uber Pool.

## 🛡 Building Trust: Perception and Guarantee

*(Section based on concepts from Alex Hormozi — $100M Leads and $100M Offers)*

### Increasing Success Perception

When someone seeks us out, they carry the weight of past experiences, skepticism, and the fear of making the wrong decision once again. The person is not just asking themselves "does this work?", but rather "**will this work for me?**".

Ten pillars to materialize trust:

1. **Your own story**: showing that you've already walked the path the person wants to travel.
2. **Others' stories**: showing that other people succeeded in accomplishing (testimonials and depositions).
3. **Proof volume**: presenting the number of positive reviews or the quantity of people you've already helped.
4. **Formal validation**: displaying certifications and training that legitimize your knowledge on the subject.
5. **Data and research**: using statistics to anchor the promise of results in evidence.
6. **Expert endorsement**: showing that other reference figures in the field attest to your credibility.
7. **Public figure endorsement**: celebrities and influencers that the person trusts and who recommend you ("they trusted them, so I'll trust too").
8. **Contextual empathy**: describing the person's situation or challenge with such a level of detail that they feel deeply understood ("they really understand me and must know how to help").
9. **A new mechanism**: presenting a unique approach or feature that the person hasn't tried yet, opening the possibility that "this time, it might work".
10. **Practical demonstration**: showing the result happening, either live or in a recording, to make success tangible.

---

### Guarantee Commitment

Trust manifests here in a deeper way: not in more gain promises, but in the **removal of loss risk**. Subtracting fear becomes more powerful than adding one more benefit.

#### Unconditional Guarantee
The most radical form of removing friction. The message: *"I'm not asking you to say yes or no today, but to make an informed decision. Both choices are risk-free, but only one of them can help you reach your purpose."* The responsibility for risk is fully absorbed by whoever offers the help.

#### Super Conditional Guarantees
Co-responsibility approach. Must be **"better than money back"** — because the person's time and energy are also valuable. Aligns commitment: *"If you do your part, I guarantee mine."* The guarantee terms mirror the essential actions for the person to succeed.

Explicitly state the minimum requirements that the person must fulfill to access the guarantee (e.g., having used the product with a minimum of Y people for so long; having participated for at least X days of training; etc.)

**Examples of super guarantee after passing minimum requirements**:
1. Excess refund (e.g., 2–3x the cost of the product/program/service)
2. Service guarantee (e.g., continue working with them for free)
3. Modified service guarantee (e.g., continue working for twice the time, for free)
4. Credit-based guarantee (e.g., give credit for other programs)
5. Personal service guarantee (e.g., you'll help them 1:1 until they achieve the result)
6. Hotel and airfare (e.g., you'll refund the program and the cost of hotel and airfare)
7. Salary payment (e.g., you'll pay their hourly rate for each hour spent with you, plus the program cost)
8. Commitment release (e.g., they don't need to remain in your contract)
9. Second deferred payment (e.g., you won't charge them again until after they make or get their first result)
10. First result guarantee (e.g., you'll continue paying their accessory expenses — ad spending, hotel, etc. — until they achieve their first result)

#### Anti-Guarantees
In contexts of high demand or rare opportunities, the absence of a guarantee can, paradoxically, signal value. The "all sales are final" policy works best for high-value services or explosive situations, where the opportunity itself is the great attraction.

#### Implicit Guarantees (Performance / Revenue Sharing)
- *Performance*: "Pay me only $X per sale you make, per kilo lost, per scheduled appointment"
- *Result sharing*: "Pay me only X% of revenue generated with the product, X% profit share, X% revenue growth relative to baseline"

#### Guarantee Stacking
- **Option A**: choose one conditional and one unconditional guarantee and combine them.
- **Option B**: choose two conditional guarantees around small and large results with different terms.
- **Option C**: choose two conditional guarantees with the same terms, but short and long deadlines to show what they could achieve.

## 🎁 Adding Value: Bonuses

### Bonus Characteristics

A bonus can be any resource that requires an initial investment of time or money to create, but that can be replicated afterward with low or no marginal cost.

Ideally, the perceived value of the **set of bonuses could exceed the value of the main solution**. Bonuses should contain elements you consider important for the person's journey, preventing useful information from being lost.

Bonuses that deliver value directly and can be easily replicated (whether digital or physical) are more sustainable.

### Types of Bonuses

- **Checklists, tools, reference files, scripts, templates, tickets for a virtual experience.**

- **Partnership with adjacent local or virtual suppliers** to offer as a bonus. Negotiate group discounts and commissions. Offer other people's products:
  - With pre-negotiated discounts or free periods/units
  - With pre-negotiated discounts or free periods/units + affiliate commissions
  - With pre-negotiated fees and exclusive resources/licenses + affiliate commissions

- **A higher service level for a determined time** (e.g., access to VIP support for one month) — whose cost to you is fixed.

- **Resolution of a next step**: anticipate and solve the next problem the person will likely encounter after using your main solution. The bonus can be this bridge to the next phase of their journey.

- **Any other resource** that would demand considerable time and effort for the person to create on their own.

## 🚀 Promotions for Launch

A simple offer, but communicated unequivocally, tends to work better than a brilliant offer that nobody understands.

### MAGIC Structure
*(Concept adapted from Alex Hormozi — $100M Offers)*

**M (Magnet → Reason)**: what is the magnetic reason for this offer to exist now? (discount, special launch condition, seasonal offer) Creates the context.

**A (Avatar → Avatar)**: who is this offer for? Naming the audience helps the person immediately identify. (small business owners? writers? those seeking a healthy lifestyle?)

**G (Goal → Purpose)**: what is the desired outcome the person can achieve? It's not a guarantee, but the direction, the purpose. (clarity in business? launching a product?)

**I (Interval → Timeframe)**: in how much time or for how long? Defines a temporal horizon that makes the promise more concrete. (30 days? 6 weeks? a single meeting?)

**C (Container → Packaging)**: what is the "packaging" of the offer? A word that gives shape to the experience and helps make it tangible. (a program, a blueprint, a workshop, a mentoring, a consulting)

---

### 4 Promotion Strategies for Launch

#### Strategy 1: The Loss Leader
Drastically reduce the entry barrier so a new customer can experience your work. You offer an initial product or service with a calculated loss, knowing that a portion of these customers will purchase higher-value solutions in the future.

- **Reason (M)**: give an accessible first step to experience a high-impact solution
- **Avatar (A)**: small business owners who need an initial boost and have a limited budget
- **Purpose (G)**: unlock a specific area of the business and know the potential of deeper work
- **Interval (I)**: visible results in the first session or within a few days
- **Container (C)**: initial consulting with 80% discount to unlock the business

*Tactical Examples*:
- **Unbundle**: offer a part of your main service (a diagnostic session) with a real and significant discount. If a full consulting costs R$ 500, offer the initial session for R$ 100.
- **Next step (upsell)**: at the end of the session, present a clear path to continue the work (full package), perhaps with a small discount for those who decide on the spot.

#### Strategy 2: Gift Card Sale
Allows generating upfront revenue and turning current customers into an acquisition channel. The person buys a service (for themselves or to gift) with perceived value much higher than the amount paid.

- **Reason (M)**: advance purchase of a future service with a big discount
- **Avatar (A)**: current customers, their friends and family
- **Purpose (G)**: gift someone (or themselves) with a transformation experience
- **Interval (I)**: the service can be redeemed within a defined period (e.g., 6 months)
- **Container (C)**: R$ 200 gift card for only R$ 50 — a gift worth four times

*Tactical Examples*:
- **Card value**: define a credit value (e.g., R$ 200) and a much lower selling price (e.g., R$ 50)
- **Limit**: to avoid cannibalizing future sales, limit the quantity per person (e.g., 3 cards per CPF)
- **Validity**: establish a deadline for redemption (e.g., 6 months) — creates slight urgency
- **Next step**: when the card is redeemed, the new customer has already had a positive experience, opening doors for complementary offers

#### Strategy 3: The Limited High-Value Package
Instead of offering a discount to everyone, create an exclusive and premium offer for the most engaged audience. Scarcity is not in the price, but in **access**.

- **Reason (M)**: access to a unique and exclusive opportunity
- **Avatar (A)**: customers who already trust your work and are ready for a larger investment in exchange for accelerated results
- **Purpose (G)**: achieve a level of result that wouldn't be possible with standard solutions
- **Interval (I)**: limited to the first 10% of the base who respond, or a fixed number of slots
- **Container (C)**: VIP gold package — a 6-month immersion for maximum results

*Tactical Examples*:
- **Premium product**: offer something you don't normally sell (individual mentoring program, close accompaniment for 6 months)
- **Proposed value**: the price must reflect the high value and exclusivity (e.g., R$ 10,000)
- **Scarcity and urgency**: limit the offer to a small number of slots (e.g., 5 people) or short time period (e.g., 48 hours). Communicate in advance to the list of most loyal customers.

#### Strategy 4: The Irresistible Freebie
Instead of giving a price discount, you **add more value to the original offer**. The perception of getting something valuable for free can be psychologically more attractive than a simple discount.

- **Reason (M)**: a "buy 1, get 2" offer for a limited time
- **Avatar (A)**: customers seeking to maximize the value of their investment and appreciate a good deal
- **Purpose (G)**: get more results for the same price, accelerating the process
- **Interval (I)**: the freebie is enjoyed together with the main purchase
- **Container (C)**: buy 1 consulting package and get 2 extra sessions as a gift

*Tactical Examples*:
- **Valuable freebie**: the freebie must have real perceived value. If you sell a package of 4 consultations, offer 2 extra sessions.
- **Price adjustment (optional)**: you can maintain the original price or make a small upward adjustment, communicating that the freebie value largely compensates (e.g., the package with freebie costs R$ 200, but the perceived value is R$ 300)
- **Differential in the freebie**: the extra sessions can include a premium service that's not in the normal package
- **Limit**: to create urgency, the offer can be limited to the first 5 customers

## 📢 Communication: Ads for Online Placement

The solution's audience can be in 5 stages according to the **Transtheoretical Model of Change**:

### 5 Stages

**1. Pre-contemplation (Ignorance)**: people who are not yet aware of the problem or that their life can be better.
- *Focus*: raise awareness about the problem and its impacts, showing how life can be better if the problem is solved
- *Communication*: highlight the benefits of a life without the problem, without directly mentioning the solution. Use stories, examples, and testimonials to generate identification and awaken interest.
- *Calls to action*: engage with your brand (like, comment, download free material, follow on social media, subscribe to receive news) to nurture the relationship and increase awareness about the problem

**2. Contemplation (Awareness)**: people who are aware of the problem and its urgencies, but haven't yet decided to seek a solution or don't know that one is available.
- *Focus*: present your solution as an answer to the audience's urgencies, showing how it can solve the problem and improve people's lives
- *Communication*: explain the benefits of your solution clearly and concisely, using testimonials, case studies, and demonstrations to prove its effectiveness
- *Calls to action*: invite the audience to learn more about the solution, offering access to informative content, webinars, free demonstrations, or initial consultations

**3. Preparation**: people who have already decided to seek a solution and are preparing to act.
- *Focus*: help the audience prepare for change, providing detailed information, clarifying doubts, and assisting in planning next steps
- *Communication*: offer educational content, step-by-step guides, planning tools, benefit calculators, and testimonials from customers who have successfully gone through the preparation phase
- *Calls to action*: encourage the next step — schedule a consultation, request a quote, or enroll in a coaching program

**4. Action**: people who are implementing the change and using your solution.
- *Focus*: offer support and encouragement so the audience stays motivated and overcomes change challenges
- *Communication*: maintain regular contact with customers, offering tips, reminders, additional resources, and personalized follow-up. Create an environment of support and celebration of achievements.
- *Calls to action*: encourage continuing to use the solution, share progress, and provide feedback on the experience

**5. Maintenance**: people who have already adopted the solution and seek to maintain results and continue evolving.
- *Focus*: retain customers, offering continuous support, relevant content, and improvement opportunities
- *Communication*: maintain regular contact through email marketing, newsletters, social media, and exclusive events. Offer loyalty programs, solution updates, and educational content to keep them engaged.
- *Calls to action*: encourage sharing experiences, providing feedback, participating in online communities, and referring your solution to other people

---

### Ad Categories by Stage

#### Pre-Contemplation Phase

**Curiosity and Intrigue**: awaken curiosity with instigating questions, impactful phrases, and surprising information. Example: "Did you know that [impactful statistic about the problem]?"

**Impactful Images**: visually striking images that represent the problem or the negative impact of not solving it, evoking emotions and generating identification.

**Myths and Truths**: debunk mistaken beliefs about the problem, presenting truthful information. Example: "Discover the truth about [myth related to the problem]."

**Provocations and Questions**: challenge the audience with questions that lead them to reflect on their behaviors. Example: "Are you satisfied with your current situation?"

**Future Scenarios**: present a positive future scenario (if the problem is solved) vs. negative (if it persists). Example: "Imagine how your life would be if you no longer had to deal with [the problem]."

**Comic Exaggeration**: use humor and exaggeration to make the problem lighter and more memorable. Create funny situations that illustrate the negative consequences. Example: "Your [problem] is so big that [exaggerated and funny situation]."

#### Contemplation Phase

**Comparison and Solution**: show how your solution stands out from alternatives. Use direct comparisons, analogies, and "before and after" examples. Example: "[Your solution] vs [alternative]: which is the best choice for you?"

**Detailed Explanations**: provide clear information about the problem and how your solution solves it. Use simple language, avoid jargon. Include data and examples.

**Practical Demonstrations**: show the solution in action through videos, tutorials, success cases, or live demonstrations.

**Creative Analogies**: use unexpected analogies and metaphors to explain the problem and solution in a clearer and more memorable way.

**Unexpected Benefits**: highlight the surprising and unexpected benefits your solution offers, exploring emotional and intangible aspects.

**Dilemmas**: present common dilemmas the audience faces, showing that you understand their difficulties. Explore the consequences of each choice. Example: "Do you prefer to continue [current negative situation] or [positive situation with the solution]?"

**Comic Exaggeration**: same tactic as pre-contemplation, adapted to the context of presenting the solution.

#### Preparation Phase

**Guides and Tutorials (How-To)**: offer practical content with step-by-step instructions. Example: "[Number] simple steps to solve the problem with [your solution]."

**Expert Interviews**: present interviews with experts who address the problem, the solution, and the benefits, increasing credibility.

**Tools and Resources**: offer free tools, resources, and materials (spreadsheets, checklists, templates, ebooks, online calculators).

**Interactive Quizzes and Tests**: create quizzes that help the audience evaluate their situation and identify their needs. Use the result to direct to the most appropriate solution.

**Dilemmas**: same tactic as contemplation, adapted to the preparation context.

#### Action Phase

**Irresistible Offers**: create special offers with mental triggers of urgency and scarcity to encourage immediate purchase (discounts, bonuses, gifts, limited deadlines).

**Stories and Testimonials**: show success stories of people who used the solution and obtained positive results. Use testimonials in text, video, or audio.

**Authority and Confidence**: highlight expertise, awards...

**Mental Triggers**: reciprocity, social proof, authority, scarcity, urgency, and novelty to influence purchase decision.

**Clear and Objective Calls to Action**: use clear, objective, and persuasive CTAs, indicating to the audience what to do next. Use action verbs and language that generates a sense of urgency.

#### Maintenance Phase

**Exclusive Content**: offer exclusive content for customers (webinars, ebooks, VIP groups, online courses).

**Support and Community**: create efficient communication channels (chat, email, phone, online forums). Encourage interaction among customers, creating an engaged community.

**Loyalty Programs**: implement programs with exclusive benefits for recurring customers (discounts, personalized offers, early access to new features).

**Challenges and Contests**: promote challenges and contests that motivate customers to continue using the solution and achieve new results. Offer prizes and recognition.

**Inspiring Customer Stories**: share stories of customers who overcame challenges and achieved significant results.

**User-Generated Content**: encourage customers to create and share content related to the solution (photos, videos, testimonials). Use this content in ads to generate social proof and authenticity.

## ♻️ Innovating the Product Lifecycle

### Why It's Important

Product lifecycle activities are important as they complement the innovation strategy by meeting success criteria in each activity. Meeting these activities allows optimizing the user's perception and experience with the solution and involved service providers.

It can be the **main** innovation strategy if the product has become a commodity in the market.

> Tony Ulwick: *"The goal is to innovate the product so that the jobs in the consumption chain disappear, that is, create a product that doesn't need to be installed, configured, interacted with, repaired, maintained, and so on. Eliminating the need to execute one or more Jobs in the consumption chain makes a product easier to use and less expensive."*

**As a general rule**: people don't want to perform these activities. They deal with it out of necessity.

**Attention**: automating or removing all activities can have negative effects. The **Ikea Effect** describes that consumers attribute disproportionate value to products they created partially or fully — a level of effort can have a positive effect. There are also people who use moments of apparently trivial activities to practice mindfulness and connect to the present moment.

### Timeline and Activities

**Pre-Contemplation**
- Recognize a need (problem or frustration)
- Seek information on how to solve

**Contemplation**
- Search for existing solutions for this specific problem

**Preparation**
- Compare solutions
- Acquire the product (order, pay, or subscribe)
- Pick up the product from the supplier or receive the product
- Check if it's the correct product
- Store the product

**Action**
- Install the product
- Learn to use the product
- Configure the product
- Test the product
- Import settings or data from previous solution
- Integrate the product with other solutions
- Prepare for use
- Use the product

**Maintenance**
- Clean the product
- Perform preventive maintenance on the product
- Repair the product
- Store the product when not in use
- Replace or update the product

**Dispose of the Product**
- Cancel, recycle, sell, donate, or discard
