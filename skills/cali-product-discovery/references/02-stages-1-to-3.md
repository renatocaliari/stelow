# Stages 1 to 3: Audience, Market, and Solutions

---

## Stage 1 🔎 — Find and Understand the Audience

**Objective**: explore the "problem space" — contexts, difficulties, and needs of people who are actively seeking to solve something. It's not to validate your idea, but to deeply understand the audience.

**Tips**:
1. If you already have a solution idea, it's still worth going through this stage to better understand the audience.
2. If you don't have time/resources, explore with AI — especially purposes that people want to accomplish and related needs.
3. Useful tools: GummySearch, AnswerThePublic, Glimpse, Google Trends, Globe.engineer.

### How to Find People — SEDIF Model

*(S=Follow, E=Entertain, D=Invest Money, I=Go/Attend, F=Frequent — 4 practical dimensions)*

**Follow**: who do they follow or which people inspire them? (friend network, work colleagues, authors and influencers on social media)

**Entertain**: how do they have fun? (hobbies, places)

**Invest**: what do they invest money and time in? (conferences, events, stores that sell resources they use)

**Frequent**: what places do they usually frequent (physical or virtual) to talk about the subject? (forums and communities — Facebook, Reddit, etc.)

### How to Create a Space to Gather People:
- **Webinar** on the topic — to register, ask for name and contact.
- **WhatsApp or Telegram group** bringing together people who want to discuss the topic (or Discord server).
- **Quiz** on the topic helping the person evaluate their knowledge — to receive the result, ask for name and email. Tools: Tally, Scoreapp, Limesurvey (open-source).

### How to Understand People:
Discover:
- What is the person's context when facing the current challenge?
- What is the person's purpose at that moment?
- What are the needs related to that purpose?

**Conduct interviews** as open conversations without pressure or fixed structure — people should feel like they're "chatting". Consult the summary of the book *The Mom Test* to learn how to have this type of conversation.

Other sources: past conversations, interviews, and surveys on the subject (company files, internet), support tickets.

**Remember**:
- Don't talk about your idea: focus on the problem you want to understand.
- Talk about past experiences, not the future. Instead of "what would you do if...?", ask "tell me about the last time you needed...".

## Stage 2 🗺 — Define the Market

**Objective**: translate learnings from stage 1 into a clear market definition — not generic, but as a group of people trying to accomplish a specific purpose.

**Market Definition (Jobs to Be Done)**:
> A **(1) group of people** who want to accomplish a **(2) functional purpose** (action or expected result).

⚠️ Focus on **functional purpose** (action or expected result), not emotional or social purposes. Emotional and social are important for improving experience and communication, but don't offer practical direction for innovation.

### Examples of Markets with Functional Purposes:
- [Music enthusiast] who wants to [listen to music]
- [Person] who wants to [find a new job]

### Segments
Segment = group of people trying to achieve the same purpose, but facing different challenges.

Examples:
- [Music enthusiast] who wants to [listen to music] [while on the street]
- [Unemployed person with long professional experience] who wants to [find a new job]
- [Person] who wants to [find a new job] [in a new city or country]

Your definition can be the broad group or directly a specific segment. Strategically, focus on 1–3 segments.

### GPN Story (Group, Purpose, Needs)

After defining the market and refining the list of needs, elaborate:

> As [group — general or segment] who wants to [purpose] [optional: context], I want a solution that makes it easier, faster, cheaper, and more accurate to: [need 1], [need 2], [need 3]...

**Example**:
> As [unemployed person with long professional experience] who wants to [find a new job], I want a solution that makes it easier, faster, cheaper, and more accurate to:
> - Identify opportunities for my experience level
> - Demonstrate the value of my experience
> - Prepare for the selection process

## Stage 3 ✨ — Define and Prioritize Solutions

**Objective**: explore and prioritize possible paths, balancing what is desirable for the audience with what is viable and strategic for the business.

**Tips**:
1. Use AI to develop solutions and strategies.
2. If exploring convenience products (commodity), evaluate how to innovate in the product lifecycle.

### What to Do (Use the GPN Story as Reference):
1. Draft an **innovation strategy**: differentiated, dominant, sustaining, discreet, or disruptive.
2. Draft **potential platforms** (the "ecosystem" or "infrastructure" behind the product).
3. Draft **business models**.
4. Draft the **main features** that will address the GPN Story.
5. Draft the **9 components for a desirable solution** (below).
6. Evaluate in three aspects: positive impact on the audience, positive impact on the business, level of risks for the business.

*"Draft" = general ideas of 2 to 3 business-product solutions. It's not to document details or bureaucratize.*

---

### 9 Components for a Desirable Solution

**1. Optimize the Functional Outcome**
Ensure that the solution delivers the best possible result in terms of functionality and performance, within the specific context of use. Understand what are the success criteria used to evaluate the job to be done functional.

**2. Reduce Effort and Sacrifice (Daily Use and Lifecycle)**
Even a functionally superior solution can be rejected if it's too difficult to use, requires too much time or sacrifice. This applies to any lifecycle activity: acquire, prepare for first use, maintain, dispose. Start by optimizing acquisition and first use. Ease of transition, use, and convenience are crucial. You can decrease effort or the perception of effort.

**3. Reduce Time to Achievement**
Time is a valuable resource. Solutions that deliver results quickly or minimize the perception of time (through predictability and visibility of progress) are more attractive — especially when urgency is high or when the user seeks instant gratification. Within this component is **TTFV — Time to First Value**.

**4. Optimize the Investment-Value Relationship**
Maximize perceived value in relation to required investment. It's not just about offering the lowest price, but justifying the cost through delivered value. Can be done in two ways: reducing monetary price (making it more accessible) or increasing perceived value (highlighting benefits, quality, exclusivity, return).

**5. Increase the Perception That One Will Succeed**
Build trust and reduce hesitation. Social proof, testimonials, and case studies show that the solution works for others, decreasing perceived risk. Especially important when the solution is new, complex, or involves significant investment (time, money, effort).

**6. Maximize the Perception of Time Well Invested**
Ensure that the user feels that the time spent interacting with the solution was valuable, productive, and rewarding in itself. Goes beyond temporal efficiency — focuses on the quality of the experience during use: meaningful engagement, learning, relaxation, clear progress, or any form of intrinsic reward.

**7. Offer Autonomy with Delimited Options**
Offer autonomy to contribute (Ikea Effect) and customize the solution, but within a clear and simplified set of options to avoid decision fatigue (Paradox of Choice). Present a curated set of contribution possibilities that are meaningful and add real value.

**8. Create Positive, Meaningful, and Memorable Personal Experiences**
Focuses on the emotional and experiential dimension. Goes beyond functionality, creating positive emotional connections: surprise, delight, exclusivity, personalization, storytelling, aesthetics. Generates lasting positive memories associated with the solution.

**9. Increase the Sense of Belonging, Being Recognized and Valued**
Addresses the human need for social connection and acceptance. Solutions that promote communities, facilitate recognition among users, or value individual contributions can generate a strong sense of belonging, increasing engagement, loyalty, and turning users into advocates.

**Complementary Elements** — in addition to the 9 components, evaluate if your solution helps people to:
- **Enjoy the moment with satisfaction** (present pleasure, not just future result)
- **Increase social influence** over issues that impact their lives
- **Increase social status**
- **Increase purchasing power** for goods and services

### Positive Impact and Level of Risk for the Business

Evaluate potential solutions in a matrix of:

**Positive Impact (Low → High)**:
- Market/niche/innovation impact
- Financial impact
- Operational impact
- Brand/reputation impact
- Social/environmental impact

**Risk Level (Low → High)**:
- Probability of failure: market risk, technological risk, execution risk, financial risk, regulatory/legal risk, operational risk, etc.
- Impact of failure: financial loss, time loss, reputation loss, opportunity loss, legal, etc.

**Rule**: start with what has very high positive impact and very low risk.
